The pricing method

Start with cost. Solve for the price.

A target margin is a share of the selling price, so it belongs in the denominator. Write the target as a decimal: 30% becomes 0.30.

Step 1

Estimate cleaner-hours

Crew size × hours on site = total cleaner-hours. Two cleaners working three hours create six cleaner-hours of labor.

Step 2

Calculate labor cost

Multiply cleaner-hours by your own fully loaded internal labor cost per hour. Use business records, not the rate you charge a client.

Step 3

Add the other costs

Add supplies, travel, job-specific fees, and a consistent allocation of overhead. Include owner labor if the owner will do the work.

Step 4

Choose and verify margin

Solve the price, apply any client-facing rounding, then calculate the realized margin again. Rounding down can put the quote below target.

Core equation Price

P = C ÷ (1 − m)
C is total estimated cost. m is target margin as a decimal. The result is the pre-tax client price.

Illustrative supplies of $18 plus travel of $12 produce $30 of other direct job cost.

Add a documented share of non-job expenses. In this example, the allocation is $30, bringing total estimated cost to $210.

Price: $300

$210 ÷ (1 − 0.30) = $300. Planned profit is $90, and $90 ÷ $300 confirms a 30% margin.

Common pricing error

Margin is not markup.

Margin divides profit by price. Markup divides profit by cost. Applying a 30% markup does not create a 30% margin.

What happens if you add 30% to cost?

$210 × 1.30 = $273. The planned profit is $63, so the margin is $63 ÷ $273 = 23.1%—not 30%.

What price creates a 30% margin?

$210 ÷ (1 − 0.30) = $300. Profit is $90 and $90 ÷ $300 = 30%.

How do margin and markup convert?

Markup = margin ÷ (1 − margin). A 30% margin corresponds to a 42.9% markup. Margin = markup ÷ (1 + markup).

What can still change the result?

Extra time, rework, omitted labor, unplanned supplies, cancellations, and costs tied to the final selling price can all change realized profit. Record the actual result after the job.

Before sending the quote

Cleaning job pricing checklist.

1. Write the scope and exclusions

Record the rooms, surfaces, add-ons, condition assumptions, frequency, access constraints, and tasks that are not included. Price changes should follow scope changes.

2. Estimate total cleaner-hours

Multiply crew size by expected on-site time. Add setup, travel, laundry, disposal, or other paid time when it belongs in your cost model.

3. Use your real internal labor cost

Use current records for wages and the employment costs your business is responsible for. Do not confuse that cost with the hourly rate shown to clients.

4. Add supplies, travel, fees, and overhead

Use a consistent method and avoid counting the same cost twice. If a cost is omitted, the calculated margin will be overstated.

5. Solve price from target margin

Calculate total cost ÷ (1 − target margin). Keep the target below 100%, and use the decimal form in the equation.

6. Recheck after rounding and add-ons

After changing the client price or adding work, calculate planned profit and margin again. Document how add-ons affect time and cost, not just price.

7. Handle taxes and terms separately

Confirm applicable tax, deposit, cancellation, and payment rules for your location and business. This guide does not provide accounting, tax, or legal advice.

8. Compare estimate with the completed job

Log actual cleaner-hours and costs. Use the variance to improve the next estimate; do not treat the target margin as a promised result.
